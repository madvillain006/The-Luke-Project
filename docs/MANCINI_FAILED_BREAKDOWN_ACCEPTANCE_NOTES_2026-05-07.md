## Core Failed Breakdown Framework

A failed breakdown is not any dip under any level. It is a trap under a meaningful low, followed by recovery and acceptance. Quality varies materially. Weak examples may be near coin-flip. Strong examples can carry much higher win rates because they trap real positioning at a real technical location.

The four quality criteria are:

1. Price must put in a significant low.
   - Gold standard: prior day's low.
   - Also valid: a major multi-hour low that produces a technically obvious rally, often 20-30+ points.
   - Also valid: a cluster of lows at the same shelf.
   - Not valid: a random small low that only produced a quick bounce.
   - Litmus test: on a 15m/30m chart, the low should visually stand out as a V-shaped pivot months later.

2. The low should occur at a technically significant spot.
   - Ideally a major trendline, horizontal support, prior breakout area, or backtest area.
   - The level should matter when looking left on the chart.

3. Price must convincingly lose the low.
   - The move should be convincing enough to run long stops and trap shorts.
   - Shallow failed breakdown: under 20 points.
   - Deep failed breakdown: over 20 points.
   - Deep failed breakdowns usually need more acceptance before entry unless volatility is extreme.

4. Price must reclaim the low, then show acceptance.
   - Golden rule: if you rush, you lose.
   - Acceptance is structure, not just clock time.
   - Shallow/high-volatility examples may need only a few minutes.
   - Deep/lower-volatility examples can need much longer, sometimes over an hour.

## Acceptance Forms

Acceptance is price showing that it wants to be at or above the significant low, not rejecting from it. It is the anti-chase filter.

Form 1: Backtest from below, reject, then return.
- Price reclaims/backtests the low from below.
- It sells off from the level.
- It returns to the level again.
- The failed selloff implies supply at the level did not follow through.

Form 2: First reclaim fails, second reclaim works.
- Price rips through the low.
- It immediately falls back below and traps premature longs.
- It then recovers the low again.
- The second reclaim is cleaner because the first attempt flushed weak longs.

Form 3: Non-acceptance protocol.
- Rare, mostly when volatility is extreme and the failed breakdown is shallow.
- Price rips through the low and quickly moves 5-10+ points above it.
- The dangerous zone is roughly 0-5 points above the reclaimed low, especially if price is slow.
- If price rips through that danger zone and remains more than about 5 points above the low, wait 2-3 minutes and consider entry higher.

## Related Setups

Level reclaim:
- Different from failed breakdown.
- It is a reclaim of a horizontal S/R shelf with action both above and below.
- It is usually slower than a failed breakdown.
- Entry still needs acceptance.

Backtest entry:
- Sequence: strong breakout, defined breakout zone, first return to that zone, buy the first test.
- Probability drops on later tests.
- This is separate from failed breakdowns but can create the significant technical spot for a later failed breakdown.

Breakdown short:
- Advanced, low win-rate, high R/R.
- Requires an obvious support/cluster.
- Ideally needs a bounce or failed breakdown immediately before shorting.
- Trigger should sit below the noise, not in the chop.
- This is not Luke Watch's primary edge and should not be added unless data shows a real edge.

Market modes:
- Mode 1: trend. Rare, under 15% of price action. One side in control, shallow pullbacks, clean level-to-level continuation.
- Mode 2: range/chop. Most price action. Traps, indecision, overtrading risk. Failed breakdowns are the core opportunity.

## Luke Watch Implications

The current strategy should not treat every level touch/reclaim equally. A higher quality Luke long should stack:

- significant low or shelf quality,
- major/support confluence,
- real flush under the level,
- acceptance after reclaim,
- no rush through slow danger-zone chop,
- proper accounting for cancellations as attempted trades.

Cancellation handling matters. If the user acts on the alert, a cancelled long is at least commission/slippage and can be a real stopped trade. Cancelled live longs should not be statistically neutral.

Refire handling should preserve the edge but avoid stale chop:

- Do not ban same-level refire entirely.
- Do require a fresh reset/acceptance condition after a cancellation.
- Same-level refire should likely require a new flush, a clear below/above reset, or a short time/structure cooldown plus stronger acceptance.

Short-side expansion should be researched separately and only added if there is a data-proven edge. The pasted framework itself says breakdown shorts are low-win-rate, advanced, and under 10% of the author's trades.
